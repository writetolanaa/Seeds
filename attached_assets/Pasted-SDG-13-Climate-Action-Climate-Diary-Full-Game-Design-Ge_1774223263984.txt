SDG 13 – Climate Action: Climate Diary – Full Game Design

Genre: Narrative + Strategy Simulation
Target Audience: Teens, casual strategy players
Tone: Educational, interactive, decision-driven

⸻

1. Core Gameplay Loop
	1.	Player reviews climate metrics: CO₂ levels, temperature, biodiversity, and ecosystem health.
	2.	Make policy and lifestyle choices that affect the environment.
	3.	Simulate one year per turn and observe climate consequences.
	4.	Respond to events: natural disasters, pollution spikes, policy challenges.
	5.	Track long-term environmental trends and global goals.
	6.	Repeat across multiple years to stabilize or improve climate conditions.

⸻

2. Step-by-Step Player Actions

Step 0: Start of Year
	•	Display global dashboard: CO₂ levels, temperature, biodiversity, energy usage, extreme weather risk.
	•	Highlight urgent areas needing intervention.

Step 1: Choose Actions
	•	Player selects from policy, economic, or lifestyle interventions:
	•	Renewable energy investment (solar, wind, hydro)
	•	Deforestation prevention and reforestation programs
	•	Public awareness campaigns (reduce consumption, recycling)
	•	Industrial emission regulations
	•	Transportation and infrastructure changes

Step 2: Allocate Resources
	•	Each intervention consumes limited resources: budget, manpower, or political influence.
	•	Player must prioritize actions based on immediate vs long-term impact.

Step 3: Simulate Year
	•	Calculate effects of interventions on CO₂, temperature, biodiversity, and extreme weather.
	•	Random events may occur: droughts, floods, wildfires, industrial accidents.
	•	Player can respond with emergency measures, adjusting resources or policies.

Step 4: Track Consequences
	•	Global metrics update dynamically:
	•	CO₂ reduction or increase
	•	Temperature changes
	•	Biodiversity gains or losses
	•	Frequency/severity of extreme weather

Step 5: End of Year / Turn
	•	Present visual and numeric feedback: graphs, animated global changes, citizen reactions.
	•	Adjust available resources and introduce new events for next year.

⸻

3. Key Variables & Objects

Global Climate Attributes
	•	CO₂ level (ppm)
	•	Average global temperature (°C)
	•	Biodiversity index (0–100)
	•	Extreme weather risk (0–100)

Policy / Intervention Attributes
	•	Name and type (energy, forest, industrial, lifestyle)
	•	Cost (budget, manpower, political capital)
	•	Effectiveness (impact on CO₂, biodiversity, or temperature)
	•	Duration / persistence

Global Variables
	•	Year count
	•	Total resources available
	•	Population awareness and compliance index
	•	Event queue (disasters, industrial incidents)

⸻

4. UI Elements
	•	Top bar: year, resources, global CO₂, temperature, biodiversity
	•	Policy panel: available interventions, cost, and expected impact
	•	World map: visual changes in forests, ice caps, pollution hotspots
	•	Alerts: extreme weather events, unexpected emissions, climate crises
	•	End-of-year report: graphs showing progress, setbacks, and long-term trends

⸻

5. Feedback Systems
	•	Visual: shrinking glaciers, deforestation, storm animations, biodiversity changes
	•	Audio: weather sounds, alerts for disasters, policy success chimes
	•	Graphs: CO₂ levels, temperature trends, biodiversity index
	•	Citizens / global population reactions: positive if policies succeed, negative if disasters occur

⸻

6. Progression
	•	Unlock advanced policies, technologies, and renewable energy options as years progress
	•	Introduce global challenges: climate treaties, industrial lobbying, natural disasters
	•	Difficulty scales: new regions or faster environmental changes
	•	Track cumulative impact for long-term global success

⸻

7. Win/Loss Conditions
	•	Win: Global metrics stabilize or improve within target thresholds over X years (CO₂, temperature, biodiversity)
	•	Loss: CO₂ exceeds critical levels, temperature rises uncontrollably, biodiversity collapses, or repeated disasters overwhelm resources
